Why Solar-Thermal Propulsion Breaks a 50-Year Tradeoff

Every spacecraft ever flown has been forced to choose: burn fast and die young (chemical) or sip propellant and take 18 months to move (electric). With over 1.2 million satellites now filed for orbit, maneuvering is becoming a continuous operational load, and neither legacy architecture can serve it. This explainer walks through the physics — one equation, one heat exchanger, one cost model — showing why concentrated sunlight is the first architecture to escape the binary.

The Logarithm That Governs Everything in Orbit

Total velocity change obeys Δv = Isp·g₀·ln(m_wet/m_dry): it grows linearly with exhaust efficiency (Isp) but only logarithmically with propellant load. Doubling propellant buys you a fixed ln(2) increment, and tank mass grows with it — so a 500 kg spacecraft on storable bipropellant (Isp ≈ 300s) hits a practical wall near 700 m/s no matter how much fuel you cram aboard. Electric thrusters at 1,500s Isp escape the wall by multiplying the whole logarithm by 5, which is why Isp, not tank size, is the lever that matters.

Isp Measures Economy, Not Tempo

Maneuver time is Δv divided by acceleration, so it scales inversely with thrust — and electric propulsion's millinewtons make it catastrophically slow regardless of its 1,500s Isp. A 100 m/s reposition takes a Hall thruster ~223 hours of continuous firing; a 5,000 m/s LEO-to-GEO transfer takes 527 days. For a defense customer facing a demonstrated adversary maneuver, an asset that arrives in 527 days did not arrive. Solar thermal (100–200 N) is the only class that stays in single-digit hours across the entire Δv range while remaining indefinitely storable on orbit.

Separate the Energy Source from the Reaction Mass

In a chemical rocket the propellant is both fuel and reaction mass, so Isp is capped by combustion enthalpy. Solar thermal breaks the coupling: deployable mirrors concentrate sunlight onto a heat exchanger that raises the propellant to ~3,000 K, and exhaust velocity then scales as √(T/M) — set by temperature and molecular weight alone, not chemistry. Choose ammonia (M = 17) and you get 350–450s; choose hydrogen (M = 2) and you get ~800s, well above any storable chemical ceiling, while thrust stays at 100–200 N because the flow is a dense thermal gas, not a trickle of ions. Note what this is not: there is no photovoltaic conversion and no electrostatic acceleration anywhere in the loop.

The Physics Was Validated in 1979 — Manufacturing Was the Bottleneck

NASA and the USAF validated solar thermal decades ago; ground tests exceeded 800s Isp with hydrogen. What killed it three times over: 1980s heat exchangers were hundreds of brazed machined parts, 1980s materials failed structurally near 1,800 K, and deployable mirrors were an order of magnitude too heavy. Since Isp scales as √(T/M), the material temperature ceiling directly gated performance — and additively manufactured refractory alloys now hold to ~3,000 K in a single monolithic part, while membrane optics deliver >1,000 W/kg versus ~100 W/kg for conventional arrays. The instinct 'if it worked, someone would have built it' inverts here: it worked, repeatedly, and was shelved on manufacturability grounds that have since dissolved.

Cheapest at Every Delta-v, Not Just at a Crossover

Cost per maneuver is not propellant price — it's three stacked layers, and they interact. Propellant: ammonia at $1/kg keeps the solar-thermal curve flat while xenon at $2,500/kg erodes electric's efficiency edge at high Δv. Amortization: with 50+ restarts across a 5–6 km/s lifetime budget, each solar-thermal maneuver absorbs a sliver of the standardized $10M hardware, whereas chemical stages concentrate full cost into a handful of burns and Hall thrusters wear out from throughput erosion. Energy: sunlight is free and external; chemical systems burn their energy supply, electric thrusters degrade as slow consumables. The result is not a crossover chart — solar thermal is the floor across the entire domain, which is what makes it a platform rather than a niche.
