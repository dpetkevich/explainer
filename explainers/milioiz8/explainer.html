How to Make Eight GPUs Act Like One: The Three Rules Behind ParallelKittens

Modern AI models are too big to fit on one chip, so their giant matrix multiplications are split across eight or more processors that must constantly trade partial results over wires. The chips themselves have gotten faster much more quickly than the wires between them, and today some models spend more than half their time just waiting for data to arrive. A new paper from Stanford shows that this waiting can be made to almost vanish — not with a heroic new machine, but with three simple design rules that fit in fifty lines of code and beat systems built by teams of specialists, sometimes by a factor of four. To see why the trick works, and why nobody's library did it already, we have to begin at the beginning: what a GPU actually is, and what it costs to move a byte.

The building blocks: one chip, many chips, and the waiting problem

Before we can hide any waiting, we need the ground truth: what a GPU is made of, why running two things at once is the only free lunch in hardware, and why the wires between chips became the slow part. This section ends with a single equation that names every place time can leak.

A GPU is 130 small computers, and every step away from them costs 10× in bandwidth

Look inside one H100 GPU and you find not one computer but about 130 independent ones, called streaming multiprocessors — SMs for short. Each SM carries its own tiny, blazingly fast memory: 64 KB of registers it can touch every clock tick (about 130 TB/s summed over the chip) and 227 KB of so-called shared memory at roughly 33 TB/s. All the SMs together share a 50 MB cache called L2 running at about 12 TB/s, and behind everything sits the 80 GB main memory, HBM, at 3 TB/s. Notice the pattern: each step away from the SM costs you roughly a factor of ten in bandwidth — and since bandwidth is just bytes delivered per second, a fixed chunk of data takes ten times longer to fetch from each tier down. The whole game of fast GPU code is keeping data next to the SMs and reaching down to HBM as rarely as possible.

Running compute and data movement at the same time turns a sum into a max

A GPU contains several distinct machines that happen to share one chip: tensor cores that do matrix arithmetic, CUDA cores for elementwise math, small data-moving engines on each SM called Tensor Memory Accelerators (TMA), and copy engines that shuttle bulk data between memories. Suppose the arithmetic takes time Tcomp and moving the data takes Tcomm. Do them one after the other and you pay Tcomp+Tcomm; do them at the same time — which is possible precisely because they run on different silicon that never fights for the same transistors — and you pay only max(Tcomp,Tcomm). Slide the transfer time below the compute time and watch it vanish entirely: the slower stream hides completely under the faster one, like a second worker on an assembly line. But be warned — this overlap is not automatic; naive code issues one operation, waits for it to finish, then issues the next, and the software must explicitly fire transfers asynchronously to earn the max.

Chips got 7× faster while the wires between them got only 3× faster

Modern AI models are dominated by enormous matrix multiplications — the field calls each one a GEMM, for general matrix multiply — and the models are too large for any single GPU, so their matrices are cut into pieces spread across eight or more devices; this cutting is called sharding. The shards must constantly exchange partial results over dedicated wires called NVLink, which carry 450 GB/s per H100. Here is the trouble: step through the hardware generations and watch the bars grow at different rates — from the A100 to the B200, tensor-core arithmetic improved 7.2× and HBM bandwidth 5.1×, but NVLink only 3× and the network cables between servers only 2×. Compute per byte of communication keeps rising, so the same model now spends a growing slice of its time — over 50% in some large-language-model workloads — with its tensor cores idle, waiting on the wires. The memory bottlenecks inside one GPU were largely tamed by data-placement-aware algorithms like FlashAttention; the wires between GPUs are the bottleneck that remains.

A multi-GPU program's runtime splits into four independently attackable pieces

One program launched onto the GPU is called a kernel, and the paper's whole argument hangs on one bookkeeping identity for how long a multi-GPU kernel takes: Tkernel=Tlaunch+max(Tcomp,Tmem,Tcomm)+Tnon-overlap+Tsync. You pay a fixed fee to start the kernel, then the three pipelines — arithmetic, memory traffic, wire traffic — run side by side so only the slowest one shows up on the clock, exactly the max-not-sum rule from before. But anything you failed to overlap gets added back as Tnon-overlap, and every handshake between devices adds Tsync. Drag the slider that controls what fraction of the communication you managed to hide, and watch the leaked term grow or vanish. The paper's three principles are simply three attacks, one per leak: choose the transfer mechanism to raise the wire pipeline's bandwidth, choose the schedule to shrink Tnon-overlap, and strip library overheads to shrink Tsync.

How the waiting is made to disappear

Armed with the cost equation, we now build the paper's machine piece by piece: how one chip reaches directly into another's memory, which of three byte-moving mechanisms to use when, two rival ways to schedule compute against communication, and the template that packages all of it into fifty lines.

One GPU can write straight into another GPU's memory through an ordinary pointer

Every program addresses memory through pointers — numbers that name locations — but each GPU's pointers normally name only its own 80 GB of HBM; point at your neighbor's memory and the hardware faults, because your virtual addresses have no idea the neighbor exists. The fix is a mapping, installed once at startup: your GPU exports a handle describing a region of its physical HBM — under CUDA's inter-process scheme this handle is a 64-byte stub — and the peer imports it, wiring a range of its own virtual addresses to your physical memory. From then on, the peer just executes an ordinary store through that pointer, and the NVLink fabric carries the bytes across transparently; no library, no handshake, no help from the CPU. Flip the toggle and try the dereference both ways: unmapped, the store faults; mapped, the bytes land in the neighbor's HBM. This one trick — set up the plumbing once, then communicate with bare instructions — is the foundation under everything the paper builds, and its utilities hide the whole operating-system dance so it never appears in user code.

The mover with the highest peak bandwidth is the slowest one for small messages

Once the mapping exists, you still must choose which machine physically pushes the bytes, and there are three. The host-launched copy engine posts the best peak — 82% of NVLink's 450 GB/s — but only when fed contiguous messages of 256 MB or more; starve it with small chunks and its throughput falls off a cliff. The Tensor Memory Accelerator, that per-SM data engine from earlier, reaches 78% of peak with messages as small as 2 KB, and — this is the crucial part — a single thread fires it asynchronously, so about 15 SMs' worth of participation saturates the whole link while the rest of the chip keeps computing. Plain register-level loads and stores are the least efficient (70–76% of peak) and need roughly 76 SMs issuing synchronously in unison, yet they alone can do things the others cannot, like asking the network switch to do arithmetic — we will cash that in two scenes from now. Drag the message-size slider and watch the ranking flip: existing systems hard-code one mechanism and pay for it everywhere, while the paper's first principle is to mix mechanisms by job.

You can split each SM between compute and communication, or dedicate whole SMs to each

To overlap compute and communication you must decide where the split lives, and there are exactly two choices. Intra-SM overlapping divides the threads inside every SM: most drive the tensor cores while a single thread fires asynchronous TMA transfers — so all 130 SMs keep doing arithmetic (compute scales with SM count; the wire does not, so donating SMs to communication buys nothing there), and the two halves synchronize through a flag in on-chip shared memory in about 64 ns. Inter-SM overlapping instead walls off whole SMs as dedicated communicators: you give up some arithmetic, and the two groups must now signal each other through HBM at about 832 ns per handshake — thirteen times slower. Why would anyone pay that price? Because a dedicated communicator SM is freed from the computation's piece-by-piece rhythm and can batch, prefetch, and reshape traffic in ways a thread embedded in the compute loop never could. Flip the strategy on this workload — a GEMM whose partial sums get added across GPUs, each GPU keeping its own slice of the total, an operation called reduce-scatter — and intra-SM wins, 743.7 versus 618.1 TFLOP/s; hold that thought, because the next scene shows a workload where the verdict reverses completely.

The network switch can add the numbers itself, cutting traffic by the number of GPUs

The GPUs in a server are not wired to each other in pairs; every NVLink runs into a shared switch chip, the NVSwitch, that routes packets between them — and it turns out the switch contains arithmetic units. Consider an all-reduce, where every one of the N GPUs must end up holding the full sum of all N partial results: done naively, every GPU writes its partials to all N peers, and since each destination has just one 450 GB/s inlet, those N incoming streams queue up single-file — total transfer time grows in proportion to N. The alternative uses a special multicast address, one virtual address that stands for physical memory on all devices at once (set up through the same mapping machinery as before), plus register-level instructions like multimem.red that tell the switch fabric: sum the N copies as they pass through you. Now each GPU sends its data roughly once and receives the finished sum roughly once, and the traffic barely notices N at all. Because only synchronous register instructions can invoke this — the occupancy-hungry mechanism from the three-movers scene — the natural schedule is inter-SM: compute SMs finish their partial sums and raise a flag, and a few dedicated communicator SMs fire one in-fabric all-reduce; that is how the verdict flips, 623.9 versus 172.3 TFLOP/s, a 3.62× win no intra-SM scheme can reach.

Once the reduction dimension passes about 2,200, communication time vanishes under compute

Here is something delightful: you can predict from a one-line formula whether a fused kernel will hide its communication perfectly. Take an M×N×K matrix multiply — K is the inner dimension the sum runs over — computed in small rectangular blocks of the output called tiles. An m×n tile costs 2mnK floating-point operations, so its compute time is 2mnK/R at tensor-core rate R; but shipping that finished tile to a peer moves only smn bytes (where s is bytes per number), taking smn/B at wire bandwidth B. The compute time grows with K and the transfer time does not — so once KgeqsR2B, each tile's transfer fits entirely under the computation of the next tile, and communication costs nothing on the clock. For 16-bit numbers on an H100 (s = 2 bytes, R = 989 TFLOP/s, B = 450 GB/s) the threshold lands at K ≈ 2,197, and the measurements agree beautifully: at K = 512 communication eats 68% of the runtime, at K = 2,048 it is down to 26%, and at K = 4,096 it is under 1%. Slide K yourself and watch the transfer bar sink beneath the compute bar.

One template with four worker roles turns the whole design space into a single knob

All this analysis becomes a usable system through eight small building-block operations plus one program template. The building blocks work on tiles laid out identically on every GPU — so a tile is addressed by its coordinates, not by which device owns it — and each one exposes only the winning mechanism for its job: single-thread asynchronous peer stores ride the TMA, in-network reductions use register instructions, and four signal/wait primitives handle synchronization. The template then assigns each warp — a group of 32 threads that execute in lockstep — one of four roles: loaders fetch tiles from memory, consumers run the tensor cores, storers write results out, and communicators are the optional dedicated SMs from the inter-SM strategy. That last role is the knob: set the communicator count to zero and you have pure intra-SM overlap; raise it and you shift toward inter-SM — and the paper shows the best setting moves with problem size, since small problems finish their arithmetic quickly and need proportionally more SMs pushing bytes. Everything a new kernel adds on top of this template is under 50 lines of device code.

Head to head with the standard toolkits

The principles are simple; the test is whether they beat the software everyone actually uses. Here we put the fifty-line kernels against NVIDIA's own communication libraries and against hand-tuned specialist systems, with the paper's measured numbers.

Standard communication libraries tax every message with handshakes and extra copies

Even with the right mechanism and the right schedule, the standard libraries leak time by design. NCCL — NVIDIA's stock collective-communication library — forces a two-way handshake on every transfer, sender and receiver confirming each other even when the sender is simply pushing data one way, and it stages every message through small pre-allocated intermediate buffers, adding a full extra copy. NVSHMEM, the standard fine-grained alternative, performs a load from slow HBM to look up the peer's address and then a whole-thread-group synchronization on every single remote access — measured at 4.5× higher latency per access than a bare mapped-pointer store. For 256 MB messages these taxes amortize into nothing, but shrink the message and they swallow the payload. The paper's kernels instead do what the peer-pointer scene set up: exchange addresses once at startup, keep them in registers, write directly into the destination buffer, and signal completion one-way — worth up to 1.79× on all-reduce for small matrices on a B200, and about 20 GB/s of extra sustained bandwidth. Press the library buttons and watch which segments of the per-message anatomy simply disappear.

The same fifty-line recipe matches or beats specialist systems across four ways of splitting a model

There are four standard ways to shard a model, each with its own communication rhythm: splitting the weight matrices (gather inputs before the GEMM, reduce partial sums after), splitting the training data, splitting long input sequences across devices for attention, and splitting a model's expert sub-networks so tokens must be routed to the right device and back. The claim is generality, so here is the scorecard from the same eight primitives. Fused GEMMs: 1.06–1.68× over the non-overlapped standard stack (cuBLAS + NCCL) and 1.07–5.63× over the Triton-Distributed compiler. Sequence-split attention: 4.08× over the specialist system xDiT — fusing communication into one kernel with dedicated communicator SMs restores on-chip cache reuse that separate communication streams destroy — and 1.01–1.39× over YunChang by replacing forced tensor-reshaping copies with a direct fine-grained exchange. Expert routing: 0.92–1.22× against Comet, a hand-tuned system built by a dedicated team, while the compiler baseline proves brittle — tuned for one chip variant, Triton-Distributed sometimes runs slower than doing no overlap at all on an H100 — and every one of these kernels is still under 50 lines of added device code.

Why this matters more every year

Finally, the trend line: each new GPU generation widens the gap between compute speed and wire speed, so the fraction of workloads that can hide their communication shrinks. That is why portable principles are becoming more valuable than chip-specific tuning.

Each hardware generation raises the bar for hiding communication, so principles beat tuning

Look back at the hiding condition KgeqsR2B and notice what sits in it: the ratio of compute rate R to wire bandwidth B. We saw that from A100 to B200 compute grew 7.2× while NVLink grew only 3×, so R/B keeps climbing — and every time it climbs, the threshold K rises with it, and the shaded region of workloads whose communication hides for free gets smaller. Drag the ratio forward and watch the region shrink: the waiting problem is not a passing quirk of today's chips, it is the direction the hardware is moving. This is also why the paper's answer is three principles rather than one heroic implementation — the same fifty-line kernels ran on both Hopper and Blackwell, while the compiler baseline, auto-tuned for a particular chip variant, sometimes ran slower on an H100 than doing no overlap at all. When the hardware shifts under you every two years, what survives is not the tuning but the reasoning: pick the mover by granularity, pick the schedule by communication pattern, and refuse to pay for handshakes you don't need.
