Intelligence per Watt: Can Your Laptop Take Over from the Datacenter?

Every question you type to an AI today gets shipped across the internet to a warehouse full of specialized chips, answered there, and shipped back — and each answer costs real, measurable joules. A new study asks a physicist's question: how much correct answering do you get per watt, and has the small machine on your desk become efficient enough to keep most of that work at home? The answer turns out to be yes for almost nine of every ten everyday questions, with total energy savings of 60–80% — even though the datacenter chip is still the better machine per query. To see why that isn't a contradiction, we have to begin at the beginning: what a watt is, what a joule buys, and what a computer physically does when a language model writes one word.

The basic building blocks: what one answer physically costs

Before any claims about AI efficiency can mean anything, we need the physics: power versus energy, what generating a word actually forces the silicon to do, and how you measure the joules. This part builds that toolkit and ends at the constraint the whole paper turns on — moving bytes, not doing arithmetic, is what an answer costs.

A watt is a rate; a joule is the bill

Suppose your laptop draws 40 watts while it thinks about a question for 10 seconds. The watts are a rate — joules flowing per second — and the bill you actually pay is the energy: E=P·τ, here 40 W × 10 s = 400 J. Drag the duration out to 60 seconds and the power meter never moves, but the bill swells to 2,400 J. Keep this distinction in your pocket: a device can look frugal on a power meter and still be a glutton for energy if it is slow. Everything in this paper — every comparison between a laptop and a datacenter — comes down to which of these two numbers you are looking at.

Every word an AI writes drags the entire model through memory

A language model is just a function with N adjustable numbers called weights — an '8B' model has 8×10⁹ of them, stored as 2 bytes each, 16 GB in all. Here is the physical fact everything else rests on: to produce one output word (one 'token', the model's unit of text), the chip must read essentially all N weights out of memory and do about 2N multiply-adds with them. Watch the sweep: each token you generate drags the full 16 GB through the silicon, and a 300-token answer drags roughly 4.8 TB. Slide the model down to 1B and each token costs only 2 GB of traffic — the cost per word is simply proportional to N. An answer is not an abstraction; it is a definite, countable amount of memory traffic.

Memory bandwidth, not arithmetic, sets the speed of an answer

You might guess an AI chip is limited by how fast it can multiply. It isn't — not for a single query. Since each token needs a full 16 GB pass over an 8B model's weights, the pace is set by the pipe between memory and the arithmetic units: token rate ≈ bandwidth ÷ bytes per token. Widen the pipe from a phone's 60 GB/s to a laptop's 546 GB/s to a datacenter chip's 8 TB/s and watch the words-per-second climb from about 4 to 34 to 500 — while the multiplier units sit mostly dark, waiting for data. This is why an Apple M4 Max is 12–15× slower per query than an H100-class machine despite drawing far less power: it is not short on arithmetic, it is short on bandwidth.

Energy per query is measured by sampling power twenty times a second

How do you actually weigh a query in joules? You ask the chip's own power sensor for a reading every 50 ms while the model runs, then add up the rectangles: E=intP(t)dt≈sumiPiΔt. Press play and watch the trace fill in — power spikes while the model reads the question, settles into a plateau while it writes, and the shaded area accumulates into the final bill. Each query is run 10 times and averaged, because thermal drift and background processes add noise. One honest caveat, and it matters for how literally to take what follows: software power counters carry 10–15% absolute error, so the paper's 2–7× comparisons are rock solid, but any difference under 10% is inside the noise.

The new tools: buying more correctness per joule

Now that you can price an answer in bytes moved and joules spent, we can meet the paper's instruments: a single score that divides correctness by power, and three levers — reading only part of the model per word, storing weights in fewer bits, and pooling many different models — that push that score up.

One number scores a model and its chip together: accuracy per watt

Accuracy alone can't tell you whether local AI is viable, because it ignores cost; power alone can't either, because it ignores usefulness. So take the ratio: mathrmIPW(m,h)=mathbbEq[mathrmacc(m,q)]mathbbEq[P(m,h,q)] — average correctness per query divided by average watts drawn while answering, for model m on chip h. Accuracy here is binary per query: an exact match on benchmark problems, or a win-or-tie against a frontier reference model for open-ended chat. Push either slider and notice the two ways to win — a smarter model raises the numerator, a leaner chip shrinks the denominator — which is exactly what will let the paper split progress into a model share and a hardware share. A cousin metric, intelligence per joule (IPJ), divides by energy = power × latency instead; hold that thought, because the two will disagree in an instructive way.

A model can store 120 billion weights but read only 20 billion per word

Remember the iron rule from before: each token costs a full read of the weights it uses. Here is the trick that bends it, called a mixture of experts: partition most of the weights into many 'expert' sub-networks and send each token through only a few of them. In this toy model there are 16 experts; slide the router from touching 2 of them to touching all 16 and watch the per-token memory traffic — and therefore the energy — scale with what's read, while the stored footprint never moves. The paper's best local model, GPT-OSS-120B, stores 120B weights but activates at most 20B per token, so it runs at roughly the cost of a dense 20B model while answering like something far larger. The catch is storage: all 120B weights must fit in memory — 128 GB of unified memory on an M4 Max, at a precision we'll meet in the next scene — which is why 'model size' is genuinely two numbers, not one.

Storing each weight in fewer bits cuts energy 3× and accuracy 2.5 points

Since energy tracks bytes moved, there's an obvious lever: store each weight in fewer bits, accepting a little rounding error — this is called quantization. Step from FP16 (16 bits per weight) to FP8 to FP4 and watch the model's footprint halve at each step, per-token traffic halve with it, and total measured energy fall 3–3.5× over the full FP16→FP4 trip — at a price of roughly 2.5 percentage points of accuracy per step. Now the crucial comparison, which you can check on screen: Qwen3-14B squeezed to FP4 scores 49.0% on the SuperGPQA benchmark, still beating Qwen3-4B at pristine FP16, which scores 48.5%. Parameter count buys more accuracy than precision does, so the winning local recipe is: take the largest model that fits, then quantize it hard. FP4 is also what lets a 120B-total model like the one from the last scene squeeze into a 128 GB laptop at all.

Twenty different models answer more questions than the single best one

Here is a fact worth sitting with: no single model comes close to the paper's coverage number, but a pool of them does. Different models — different training data, architectures, post-training — fail on different questions, so imagine a selector (the field calls it a router) that sends each question to whichever pooled model is most likely to solve it. Watch the grid: add the best single model and 71 of these 100 query-dots light up; add a second, differently-trained model and dots the first one missed start lighting too; by twenty models the union reaches ≈89%, a gain of 16.3 points over the best individual (GPT-OSS-120B at 71.4%) and 28.8 points over Qwen3-14B. Be careful what this does and does not say: it does not mean any small local model is smarter than GPT-5 — it means twenty complementary specialists, given a good selector, cover more ground than one generalist, and selecting the best of 20 locals even beats selecting the best of 3 frontier cloud models on three of four benchmarks. That is pure best-of-N: more draws per query, more chances to win.

Head to head with the datacenter

The levers exist; now the measurements. This part puts a laptop against datacenter silicon with the paper's own numbers: what fraction of a million real queries the laptop can cover, where the big chips still win and by how much, and why the system-level winner is not the per-query winner.

Local models can already answer 88.7% of real queries — but not all kinds

Does that pooling trick survive contact with reality? The paper tested it on one million real queries — 500K actual chat prompts from the WildChat dataset, 500K reasoning problems from NaturalReasoning, plus two hard academic benchmarks — and the pooled local models cover 88.7% overall. But press the buttons and watch the bar swing: chat and creative writing exceed 93–98%, which matters because 77% of real ChatGPT usage is exactly that — practical guidance, information seeking, and writing — while graduate-level architecture, engineering, and physical-science reasoning drops to 40–60%. So the honest statement is two-sided: the bulk of everyday demand is already locally serviceable, and a stubborn technical tail is not. And this coverage did not appear from nowhere — the best single local model went from answering 23.2% of this workload in 2023 to 48.7% in 2024 to 71.3% in 2025, rising in lockstep with the efficiency gains we'll chart later.

Per query, datacenter chips are still 1.4–7.4× more efficient

Now the other side of the ledger: give the datacenter its due. Run the very same model on three chips, one query at a time on each — no tricks, no sharing — and switch between them. Against the Apple M4 Max, an NVIDIA B200 delivers 1.40× the intelligence per watt and 1.6–2.3× per joule; SambaNova's SN40L reaches 1.78× per watt and a striking 6.5–7.4× per joule. The cause is architectural, and you already have the tools to read it off the spec panel: the datacenter chips carry stacked high-bandwidth memory at 4.8–8 TB/s and 400–1000 W thermal budgets, single-purpose machines for exactly this job, while the laptop's 546 GB/s unified memory must also serve the CPU and graphics under a thin-chassis heat ceiling. Notice the per-joule gap always exceeds the per-watt gap — the cloud chip is both leaner and faster, and speed compounds into energy. File that asymmetry away; it becomes a theme in the final part.

Serving 64 users at once cuts energy per answer 12–20×

Recall the dominant cost: reading all the weights from memory once per token. Now suppose 64 users' queries arrive at once — the chip can read the weights a single time and apply them to all 64 token-streams before the data goes cold, so that one expensive read is split 64 ways. Slide the batch size up and watch it happen in the measurements: Qwen3-8B on a B200 drops from 6,449 J per query at batch 1 to 548 J at batch 64, while total power rises only from 255 W to 883 W — far less than 64-fold — for an 11.8–19.7× gain in intelligence per joule. This sharing trick is called batching, and it is a structural asymmetry, not a clever benchmark: a datacenter always has strangers' queries to bundle, while your laptop serves one person and has nothing to batch across except, perhaps someday, its own apps. Keep this in mind for what comes next — it is exactly why the paper's cloud baseline runs batched, to avoid stacking the deck for the laptop.

A selector that is right 80% of the time captures most of the savings

The last scene assumed a perfect selector; no real one exists, so how good must the router actually be? Slide its accuracy and read the curve: at 60% correct decisions you already save 48.4% of total energy, at 80% you save 64.3%, and perfection tops out at 80.4% — each extra point of router accuracy buys less than a point of savings. The reason the floor is so forgiving is that a misroute isn't a wrong answer; the query simply falls back to the big cloud model, so answer quality is preserved and only some joules are wasted. Published routing systems (RouteLLM) already report 70–85% accuracy, so routing is not the bottleneck. The real frontier shows in the difficulty slices: the hardest reasoning tier — 16.5% of the reasoning set, beyond every model evaluated — remains 95% unsolved and improved only +1.5 points from 2023 to 2025, while the easier tiers jumped 24–76 points.

Why now: crossing the efficiency threshold

The final step is the trend line. The same measured progress reads very differently per watt than per joule, and once you can read both correctly, the paper's historical argument snaps into place: computing migrates to the small machine when it becomes efficient enough — and that threshold is being crossed right now.

The same two years of progress read 5.3× per watt but 18× per joule

Here is a puzzle you can now solve with scene one's toolkit: the paper reports that models drove recent progress (3.1× of the 5.3× per-watt gain, versus 1.7× from hardware) and that hardware drove it (5.9× of an 18× per-joule gain over 16 months) — and both are true. A new chip does two things at once: it draws somewhat less power per unit of accuracy, and it finishes each query much faster. Slide the speedup and watch: the per-watt gauge doesn't budge, because mathrmIPW=mathrmacc/P contains no time at all, while the per-joule gauge climbs in proportion, because energy is P·τ and the shrinking τ multiplies straight in. Which metric you should care about depends on your constraint: a laptop lives under a thermal ceiling, a hard cap on watts, so per-watt governs what it can sustain; a battery or an electric bill is a budget in joules, so per-joule governs what a day of use costs. Two honest metrics, one dataset, no contradiction.

Computing moves to the small machine when it becomes efficient enough, not when it wins

One last question: why is this happening now, and should we expect it to continue? History offers a control experiment: from 1946 to 2009, computations per joule doubled every ~1.5 years (a trend named for Jonathan Koomey), and work migrated from room-sized mainframes to desktop PCs not when PCs outperformed them — they never did at the time — but when a wall-socket machine became efficient enough to satisfy a typical user's needs. That is precisely a per-watt threshold, which is why this paper tracks a ratio rather than raw benchmark scores. Drag the year and watch both curves: local intelligence per watt compounding at ~2.3× per year (2.27× in 2024, 2.32× in 2025), while the share of real queries a single best local model can service climbs 23.2% → 48.7% → 71.3%, with the pooled figure at 88.7% — and the old storage barrier gone, since consumer accelerator memory has grown 126× since 2012. The datacenter keeps the hard 11%: the graduate-level reasoning tail, and every workload that batching makes cheap. But if these curves hold for even a few more years, the default answer to 'where does an AI query run?' quietly changes from 'a warehouse somewhere' to 'the machine in front of you.'
