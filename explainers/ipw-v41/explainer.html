Intelligence per Watt: When Your Laptop Should Answer Instead of a Data Center

Every question you type into a chatbot is shipped to a warehouse of GPUs and shipped back. On one million real queries, a handful of laptop-sized models together answered 88.7% correctly. The paper scores every answer with one ratio: accuracy divided by power draw, intelligence per watt. For local models it improved 5.3× between 2023 and 2025.

The basic building blocks

Before we can score any answer, we need to know what answering physically costs. We follow one query — 'explain why ice floats,' a typical single-turn chat question — through a laptop's chip, and end on the problem: watts alone can't tell you whether local AI is good enough.

Generating an answer means re-reading the whole model for every word

Each new token of the ice-floats answer forces another full read of all 16 GB of weights, so a 500-token answer moves about 8 TB through memory. Answer length, not question difficulty, is what the hardware bills you for.

Memory bandwidth, not arithmetic speed, sets tokens per second

Every token re-reads 16 GB, so the pipe from memory is the bottleneck while the arithmetic units sit idle. That's why an iPhone is ~9× slower than a MacBook on the same model.

Energy per query is power times latency: E = P·τ

Faster memory shortens latency τ — the time to produce the answer, not the answer itself. The equation Equery=P·τ means a chip that halves both power and time is 2× better per watt but 4× better per joule.

The new technology

The physics of the last part said cost is set by bytes moved per token. This part introduces the paper's metric — accuracy per watt — and the two engineering tricks (sparse expert routing and 4-bit weights) that let a 120-billion-parameter model run on a laptop at small-model cost.

Intelligence per watt scores smartness and power draw in one ratio

Joules told us what an answer costs but nothing about whether it's right — this ratio fixes that by putting accuracy in the numerator and watts in the denominator. The 2025 champion pair, GPT-OSS-120B on the M4 Max, scores 4.18×10⁻³ accuracy per watt across 1M real queries.

Mixture-of-experts models pay per token for active parameters, not stored ones

GPT-OSS-120B stores 120B parameters but activates at most 20B per token — exactly the frugality that intelligence per watt (IPW) rewards. Toggle the architecture and watch which weight blocks light up: each word of the ice-floats answer costs like a 20B model, backed by 120B of stored knowledge.

4-bit weights cut energy 3–3.5× and cost only ~2.5 points of accuracy per step

Generation is memory-bound, so dropping weights from 16-bit to 4-bit precision cuts energy 3–3.5×. Accuracy falls only ~2.5 points per halving of precision (16→8→4 bit), so Qwen3-14B at 4-bit (FP4) scores 49.0% on SuperGPQA, edging out Qwen3-4B at 16-bit (FP16) with 48.5%.

Why it beats existing tech

Now we have a fair scoreboard and a capable local model. This part runs the head-to-head against the cloud on 1M real queries: where local models win, where they still fail, why cloud silicon is still more efficient per query — and why routing queries between the two saves 60–80% of total energy anyway.

One local model answers 71.4% of real queries; the local ensemble covers 88.7%

GPT-OSS-120B alone answers 71.4% of the 1M queries; adding more local models lifts the union of correct answers to 88.7%. Their error sets only partially overlap, so diversity buys what scaling one model doesn't.

Chat is nearly solved locally; the hardest reasoning tier is 95% unsolved

That 88.7% union hides a steep gradient: coverage falls from 88.9% on chat (WildChat) to 64.9% on hard reasoning (NaturalReasoning). The hardest 'Level 5' tier — 16.5% of the reasoning set — stays 95% unsolved locally.

On identical models, cloud chips still beat the M4 Max by 1.4–7.4× per query

Same models, one query at a time: M4 Max vs cloud silicon. The B200 wins 1.4× on IPW and 1.6–2.3× per joule, and SambaNova's SN40L reaches 7.4× per joule, because 4.8–8 TB/s of high-bandwidth memory beats 546 GB/s of shared laptop memory.

Batching lets cloud servers split one weight-stream across many users

Slide the batch size: Qwen3-8B's per-query energy on a B200 falls from 6,449 J to 548 J at batch 64, while power rises only 255 W → 883 W. The paper scores the cloud baseline at batch 16, a realistic serving load — a discount a batch-1 laptop never gets.

Routing easy queries to small local models cuts system energy 60–80%

Simulating 80.2M queries/day, a perfect router saves 80.4% of energy against the batch-16 cloud baseline; a realistic 80%-accurate router still saves 64.3%. The win comes from replacing each pass through the 235B-parameter Qwen3-235B with a ≤20B-active local model.

Why now

The routing result only pays off if local models can handle most traffic — and two years ago they couldn't. This part shows the trend that changed the answer: intelligence per watt is compounding at roughly 2.3× per year, the same kind of curve that once moved computing from mainframes to PCs.

Intelligence per watt improved 5.3× in two years, from compounding model and hardware gains

IPW climbed from 7.92×10⁻⁴ in 2023 (Mixtral-8x7B on a Quadro RTX 6000) to 4.18×10⁻³ in 2025 (GPT-OSS-120B on M4 Max), a 5.3× gain. Coverage rose in step: 23.2% → 48.7% → 71.4%.
