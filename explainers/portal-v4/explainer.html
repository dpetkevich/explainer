9 Hours vs. 527 Days: How Focused Sunlight Breaks Spaceflight's Oldest Tradeoff

Over 1.2 million new satellites were filed for orbit in the last 18 months. Every one of them will need to move — dodge debris, change altitude, reach a servicing depot. For 50 years, engines forced a binary choice: move fast and die young, or move efficiently at a glacial pace. This explainer follows one job: pushing a 500 kg spacecraft from low Earth orbit to geostationary orbit, a 5,000 m/s velocity change we'll call the GEO run. One equation, ve∝T/M, lets an engine heated by mirrors do it in 9 hours. The most efficient existing thruster needs 527 days.

The trap: why moving in space is expensive

Before the new engine makes sense, you need the three rules every spacecraft obeys: thrust is thrown mass, maneuvers have fixed prices, and propellant costs grow exponentially. Together they create a 50-year-old dead end.

A rocket moves by throwing mass overboard, and the mass runs out

There is nothing to push against in vacuum: the spacecraft accelerates only by expelling its own propellant, with force F=dotm·ve. Watch the tank — when it empties, no amount of onboard power can change the trajectory.

Every maneuver has a fixed price in velocity change, set by orbital mechanics

Changing orbit means changing velocity, and physics fixes the price no matter which engine pays it: ~100 m/s for a collision dodge, ~5,000 m/s for the full GEO run. Our spacecraft's ΔV budget is loaded at launch — when the meter empties, the vehicle is dead weight.

Propellant needs grow exponentially with ΔV, so low exhaust velocity hits a hard ceiling

The logarithm in ΔV=ve·ln(mwet/mdry) is a tax: added propellant must itself be accelerated, so requirements grow exponentially. At ve = 2,900 m/s the GEO run demands an 82%-propellant vehicle — and realistic storable-propellant tanks cap our spacecraft near 700 m/s total.

The new engine: sunlight as the heat source

The trap has one exit: raise exhaust velocity without combustion. Here's the physics that permits it, and the hardware — mirrors, a heat exchanger, a nozzle — that builds it.

Exhaust velocity scales as √(T/M) — combustion locks both knobs, external heat frees them

Kinetic theory sets molecular speed at kT/m: hotter and lighter both mean faster, so ve∝T/M. Combustion welds the two knobs together — burning fixes the temperature and produces heavy exhaust molecules. Heat the propellant externally instead, and you can pick pure hydrogen (M = 2) at 3,000 K for ~800 s Isp.

Mirrors focus sunlight onto a heat exchanger — the sun replaces the combustion chamber

Follow the energy: deployable mirrors concentrate sunlight onto a monolithic heat exchanger. Ammonia flashes to ~3,000 K, and the nozzle turns that heat into 100–200 N of thrust at 350–450 s Isp. This is not solar panels driving an ion thruster — the sunlight becomes heat directly, which is why the thrust is a million times higher.

Head to head on the GEO run

Now we race. Same 500 kg spacecraft, same 5,000 m/s job, three engines — compared on time, cost, and readiness, with the paper's actual numbers.

Thrust sets the clock: the GEO run takes 9 hours, not 527 days

Maneuver time scales inversely with thrust: at 100 m/s, solar-thermal burns for ~10 minutes while electric spirals for ~223 hours. Slide to the full 5,000 m/s GEO run and the gap becomes 9 hours versus 12,600 hours — 527 days, during which the reason for moving has long expired.

Cheap propellant, reusable hardware, free energy: solar-thermal wins on cost at every ΔV

Speed usually costs money; here it doesn't. Ammonia runs $1/kg versus xenon at $2,500/kg, the hardware amortizes over 50+ restarts, and sunlight is free. The result: solar-thermal is cheapest at every ΔV — $222k vs $1.46M against chemical at 100 m/s, $9.1M vs $10.9M against electric on the full GEO run.

Why 1980 failed and 2026 works

The physics was validated in 1979 and then shelved for four decades. Three technologies had to mature — and a million-satellite sky had to create the demand.

The physics passed in 1979; the manufacturing, materials, and mirrors passed in the 2020s

Rockwell validated the physics in 1979 — ground tests even demonstrated >800 s Isp with hydrogen — but the hardware was unbuildable. Flip each subsystem from 1980 to 2026. The temperature ceiling alone rises from 1,800 K to 3,000 K; since ve∝T, that is a 29% Isp gain before the other two fixes even land.

1.2 million filed satellites turn maneuvering from an edge case into infrastructure

Responsive ΔV was never built because it was never needed: ~15,000 active satellites mostly parked in fixed slots for 15 years. Filings in the last 18 months exceed 1.2 million — over 95,000 broadband satellites, SpaceX's 1,000,000-satellite compute filing, Blue Origin's 51,600. At that density, every spacecraft becomes our scene-2 spacecraft, dodging and repositioning continuously.

Nuclear-thermal is the same engine with a denser heat source

Flip the heat source and nothing downstream changes: mirrors out, reactor in, and the same 3D-printed heat exchanger and ve∝T/M physics keep working — now free of the sunlight-availability constraint. Solar-thermal today delivers ~90% of nuclear-thermal performance at ~10% of the cost, while building exactly the flight heritage a 2030s nuclear engine will need.
