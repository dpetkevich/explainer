How to Make Eight GPUs Act Like One: The Art of Hiding Communication

Training a large AI model means splitting one giant matrix multiplication across many GPUs. Then you pay for it: the partial results must be shipped and summed over links roughly 7× slower than each GPU's own memory. In some large-model workloads, GPUs now sit idle more than half the time waiting on those transfers. This paper's fix is not faster wires but smarter scheduling: run the transfer *during* the math. Then total time becomes the maximum of the two, not their sum. Follow one split matrix multiply across 8 H100 GPUs and watch its communication cost drop from 68% of runtime to under 1%.

The basic building blocks

We set up one concrete workload — a single matrix multiply split across 8 GPUs — and meet the hardware it runs on. By the end, we see why the split creates a cost that only grows with each GPU generation.

Splitting a matrix multiply across GPUs creates a mandatory summation step

Our running example: an 8192×8192×8192 matrix multiply, its weights split 8 ways. Step the split and watch a new obligation appear — 8 partial results, 134 MB each, that must be added together across machines.

Inside a GPU, every step down the memory hierarchy costs an order of magnitude in bandwidth — and the GPU-to-GPU link is the bottom rung

Before the 134 MB sum can hurt us, see where it travels. Sweep down the pyramid: registers move data at 130 TB/s, but the NVLink pipe between GPUs manages 0.45 TB/s — nearly 300× slower.

Each GPU generation makes the math faster than the pipe, so waiting on transfers eats a growing share of runtime

Step through three GPU generations and watch our matmul's timeline: the green compute segment shrinks 7.2×, the red transfer segment only 3×. Faster hardware makes the waiting problem worse, not better.

The new technology

The paper's core move: overlap the transfer with the math so time is a max, not a sum. Then three design choices — which transfer engine, which scheduling, which library plumbing — decide whether the overlap actually works, and all three compress into eight primitives and one kernel template.

If transfers run during the math, total time is the maximum of the two, not their sum

The tensor cores and the transfer engine are separate hardware — they can run at once. Flip the toggle and watch the red bar slide underneath the green one: sum becomes max.

One inequality decides whether communication vanishes: KgeqsR/2B

Drag the inner dimension K and watch each tile's transfer slide under its compute. For BF16 on H100 the crossover is K≈2198 — and measured non-hidden communication falls from 68% at K=512 to under 1% at K=4096.

You can overlap inside every SM or dedicate whole SMs to communication — and each wins for different workloads

Toggle the schedule on a map of all 132 SMs. Intra-SM wins GEMM + reduce-scatter (743.7 vs 618.1 TFLOP/s), but inter-SM wins GEMM + all-reduce by 3.6× (623.9 vs 172.3) — no single schedule wins both.

The switch between the GPUs can do the addition itself, cutting traffic roughly 8-fold

Here is why inter-SM won all-reduce by 3.6×. Toggle: naively, 7 partial sums queue at each GPU's single 450 GB/s inlet; with in-network reduction, the switch adds them in flight and delivers one result.

Standard communication libraries pay a handshake-and-staging tax on every transfer

Even a perfect schedule loses if each transfer drags this baggage. Step through one 128 KB tile transfer: NCCL takes four stops, ParallelKittens takes one — worth up to 1.79× on all-reduce and 4.5× lower latency.

Why it beats existing tech

Prior systems each hard-coded one point in this design space. Because ParallelKittens can pick the right mechanism and schedule per workload, it beats hand-tuned specialists and compilers head-to-head on their own benchmarks.

Choosing the right mechanism per workload beats systems that hard-coded one choice

The scoreboard on 8×H100s: up to 2.33× over hand-tuned Flux, 4.08× over xDiT on ring attention, 1.22× over Comet, 1.07–5.63× over the Triton Distributed compiler. Shrink the matrix and watch the baselines fall below doing nothing at all.

Why now

The compute-versus-interconnect gap is widening with every GPU generation, so overlap becomes more valuable, not less. The same 50-line kernels ran unmodified on the next architecture and won again.
