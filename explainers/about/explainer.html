How a Protein Model Learned to Predict and Design Biology

A useful medicine often begins with a molecule that can grip one chosen target and ignore the rest. Finding that molecule has usually meant making and testing enormous collections in a laboratory. Biohub reports a system that moves much of that first search into a computer, then checks the winners in the physical world. To understand what changed, we have to begin with the tiny chains that do most of life's molecular work.

The basic building blocks

First we need the causal chain from a protein's building blocks to its shape, then from shape to binding. That makes the old laboratory search bottleneck concrete.

A protein's sequence sets the shape it can fold into

To understand protein design, begin with the object being designed. A protein is a chain assembled from 20 kinds of chemical building block called amino acids; the ordered list is its sequence. Attractions and repulsions among those building blocks pull the chain into a three-dimensional fold. Change the selected amino acid and watch the chain settle into a new contact pattern, which is why sequence is the first cause in this story.

Matching surfaces make a protein bind its target

A fold matters because shape decides what the protein can touch. Suppose one protein presents a pocket and another molecule, the target, presents a matching ridge. Where their surfaces approach closely, many weak chemical contacts can add up to a firm grip; a protein chosen for that grip is called a binder. Move the match from poor to close and watch scattered contacts become a stable interface.

Rare binders force laboratories to test enormous libraries

Now we can name the old bottleneck: the right shape is rare. A classical screen makes a physical library, meaning a large collection of candidate molecules, then tests them against the target. Set how often a hit occurs and watch the expected number of tests swell from thousands to millions. At the hard end, the first hits can take weeks or months, while development beyond that first hit typically takes years.

How the new protein machine works

Once sequence, shape, and binding are linked, we can see how a machine learns those links from examples and uses them to predict and design.

Neighboring amino acids help the machine fill a sequence gap

The machine needs a way to learn from sequences without a teacher labeling every fold. During training, the learning process, one amino acid is hidden and the machine must guess it from the neighbors. This fill-in-the-blank system is called a protein language model because it treats amino-acid sequences much as a text model treats sentences. Reveal more surrounding positions and watch a flat choice among 20 possibilities sharpen around the amino acid that fits this context.

More training produces a richer internal picture of biology

Good guesses are useful only if they teach the machine something deeper. As training grows, the numbers the model builds for each amino acid capture shape and function more accurately; that numerical summary is called a representation. The measured improvement follows a smooth trend called a scaling law, so more computing effort has a predictable payoff. Slide from millions of training sequences toward ESMC's roughly 2.8 billion and watch independent biology probes climb along the same trend.

Repeated passes refine one structural prediction

A learned representation still has to become a three-dimensional structure. ESMFold2 sends an evolving shape guess through the same machinery several times, a method called looped refinement. Each pass can correct conflicts left by the previous pass, so a hard protein can receive more computing effort without adding a larger set of machinery. Increase the loop count and watch one rough fold settle while the model-size indicator stays fixed.

A larger virtual search gives the model more chances to find a binder

Prediction answers what shape a sequence will make; design asks which sequence to try. ESMFold2 turns design into a search by generating candidate sequences, predicting how each sits on the target, then ranking them with a confidence score, the model's estimate that the pose is stable and well formed. Increase the candidate pool and the fixed toy search reveals more ranked options, so its best available score can rise but never fall. In the reported runs, generation took roughly two days and ranking took less than a day, with no special retraining for these targets.

Why it beats the old search

The mechanism is only useful if it survives real measurements. Now we compare its structure predictions, speed, designed binders, and physical geometry with laboratory evidence.

Related sequences raise correct complex predictions from 71% to 77%

The first test is whether the predicted shapes are right often enough to trust. ESMFold2 can start from one sequence because the training stage already captured evolutionary patterns. Give it an optional stack of related sequences, called a multiple sequence alignment, and correct general complex predictions on Foldbench rise from 71% to 77%. The same benchmark suite includes the harder antibody-target task, where the model predicts 55% correctly; antibodies are proteins whose flexible gripping loops make the exact pose difficult.

A 1024-amino-acid structure can be predicted in 9.4 seconds

Accuracy alone would not replace a slow search, so the next constraint is time. Protein length means the number of amino acids in its chain, and longer chains give a predictor more work. Stretch the sequence from 128 to 1024 amino acids and watch the work indicator grow while the measured endpoint remains within a few seconds. ESMFold2-Fast predicts the 1024-amino-acid case in 9.4 seconds, fast enough to make large virtual searches plausible.

More virtual search produces more binders that work in the lab

A confidence score is only a promise until molecules are made and tested. The team searched at two computing scales, then screened the top 84 designs for every target, design format, and scale across five disease-relevant targets. Raise the search compute and minibinder success climbs from 54% to 70%. The harder single-chain antibody fragments, called scFvs, climb from 12% to 21%. Because the same testing rule is used at both scales, the rising lab bars show that extra virtual search buys real experimental success.

What 4.3 nanomolar means: how tightly a binder grips

How do we say, in a number, how firmly one molecule holds onto another? We quote a dissociation constant, written KD: the concentration at which half of the target sites are occupied. The smaller that concentration, the tighter the grip. Biohub reports KD=4.3 nanomolar — billionths of a mole per liter — for its binder against PD-L1, a protein on cells that normally tells the immune system to stand down. Slide the binder's concentration and watch the share of occupied targets climb; at 4.3 nanomolar it crosses one half. Needing only so slight a concentration to fill half the sites is exactly what 'tight' means.

The observed EGFR binder pose is within 1.204 Angstroms of the prediction

A binder can grip strongly for an unexpected reason, so the final test asks whether its physical pose matches the prediction. The team used cryo-electron microscopy, which reconstructs a three-dimensional structure by averaging many electron-beam images of rapidly frozen molecules. Pose error is summarized by root-mean-square deviation, or RMSD: the typical distance between corresponding atoms after the two structures are aligned. Set the error to the measured 1.204 Angstroms and the predicted and observed EGFR minibinders nearly coincide, about an atom-width apart. Increase the counterfactual error and watch the overlay and target contacts visibly fail.

Why this is possible now

Fast prediction and learned biological coordinates can now be applied across billions of sequences. The result is not just a faster answer to one question, but a map for finding new questions.

Learned biology connects proteins that sequence spelling misses

Once billions of sequences can be represented and many structures can be predicted quickly, the next need is navigation. ESM Atlas places proteins by the model's learned biological representation rather than only by shared sequence letters. Shift the coordinate rule from raw sequence similarity toward learned biology and watch distant Fanzor and TnpB proteins join the same functional neighborhood despite low sequence similarity. The full Atlas contains 6.8 billion sequences and 1.1 billion predicted structures. That scale turns the vast unstudied collection into a place where a known neighborhood can point toward an unknown protein worth testing next.
