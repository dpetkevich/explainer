Portal Space's Solar-Thermal Satellite Propulsion

For fifty years, every satellite has faced the same forced choice. Engines with real thrust drain their tanks after a few hundred meters per second of maneuvering. Efficient electric thrusters sip propellant, but a big orbit change takes over a year of continuous firing. This explainer follows one satellite through one maneuver: a 5,000 m/s climb from low Earth orbit to geostationary orbit. The trap is not a law of physics. A thermal rocket's exhaust velocity depends only on gas temperature and molecular weight. It does not care where the heat came from. Feed cheap ammonia through a heat exchanger cooked to 3,000 K by concentrated sunlight, and the maneuver that costs an electric thruster 527 days takes 9 hours.

The basic building blocks

Meet the running example — a 500 kg satellite that must buy a 5,000 m/s orbit change — and the three pieces of freshman mechanics that price it: momentum exchange, the rocket equation, and the power budget. Together they explain why every existing engine sits in one of two bad corners.

Every orbital maneuver has a fixed price in velocity change, set by geometry alone

In orbit there is no friction, so moving between orbits costs a definite velocity change — written Δv — no matter which engine you use. Our running example, the GEO sprint, carries a fixed bill of 5,000 m/s.

Thrust is momentum thrown overboard: F = ṁ·vₑ, but power grows as vₑ²

To spend that 5,000 m/s bill, the satellite throws mass backward: thrust is F=dotm·ve, where dotm is kilograms of propellant expelled per second and ve is the exhaust velocity in m/s. At a fixed power budget F=2P/ve, so thrust and exhaust velocity trade directly.

Δv grows only as the logarithm of propellant mass: the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation

Thrust says how hard the engine pushes; this equation says how much total Δv the tanks can buy: because the rocket hauls its own unburned propellant, Δv=veln(mwet/mdry), where mwet is the fully fueled mass and mdry the empty-tank mass. Adding tanks pays off only logarithmically; raising ve pays off linearly.

On storable chemical propellant, the 500 kg satellite hits a wall near 700 m/s

Now apply the rocket equation to our satellite's 5,000 m/s bill. Before you drag the slider, guess how much of the wet mass must be propellant at chemical ve≈2.9 km/s. Then watch the curve — and watch what happens when ve rises instead.

Fifty years of engines occupy two opposite corners: high thrust or high Isp, never both

The rocket equation says raise ve; the power equation says raising ve at fixed power kills thrust. Isp — specific impulse — is just exhaust velocity restated in seconds (ve divided by g). Before you press each engine's button, predict: can it finish the 5,000 m/s GEO sprint at all, and if so, how long does it take?

The new technology

The trap in Part 1 came from letting the propellant supply its own energy. Solar-thermal propulsion cuts that link: sunlight provides the heat, so the propellant can be chosen purely for its physics — and ve∝T/M does the rest.

For a thermal rocket, exhaust velocity depends only on T/M: hot gas, light molecules

Here is the loophole in the 50-year binary. A thermal rocket is any engine that heats a gas and expands it through a nozzle. For every one of them, ve∝T/M: only the gas temperature T and its molecular mass M matter, not where the heat came from.

Pick a light working fluid, push the temperature, and Isp doubles past chemical's ceiling

Mirrors, a heat exchanger, and a nozzle: sunlight heats the propellant directly

Deployable mirrors focus sunlight onto a heat exchanger. Ammonia flows through at ~3,000 K and the hot gas expands out a nozzle at 100–200 N. This is not solar-electric — toggle the comparison and count the conversion steps each design requires.

Why now

The physics was validated in ground tests decades ago, so the real question is what changed. Three subsystem breakthroughs made the hardware buildable, a filing boom is making maneuvering routine, and the same heat exchanger is the core of the next engine after this one.

Three subsystems that were unbuildable in 1980 now exist off the shelf

Ground tests exceeded 800 s Isp decades ago. What killed the idea in 1980 was three missing subsystems — step through what replaced each. The materials panel is worth checking with the equation you already have: since ve∝T, raising the limit from 1,800 K to 3,000 K raises ve by 3000/1800≈1.29.

Filings for 1.2 million satellites turn maneuvering from a rare event into infrastructure

Cheap fast Δv only matters if satellites actually need to move — so look at the pipeline, which is regulatory filings, not deployed hardware. Even a fraction of 1.2 million filed satellites means routine collision-avoidance burns, repositioning, and end-of-life de-orbit.

Swap the mirror for a reactor and the same hardware becomes a nuclear-thermal rocket

One last consequence of ve∝T/M: the equation never asked where the heat comes from. Toggle the heat source: the heat exchanger, propellant handling, and nozzle carry over unchanged to a nuclear-thermal engine. What remains is a 2030s regulatory question, not a physics one.
