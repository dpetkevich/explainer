Distilling a Giant Network into a Small One

The most accurate systems for recognizing speech and images are enormous — or worse, they are committees of many big networks whose answers get averaged. They win benchmarks, but running ten huge networks for every voice query is far too slow and costly to deploy. This paper makes a startling claim: nearly all of that skill can be poured into one small, fast network, because the skill lives not in the millions of learned parameters but in the full pattern of probabilities the big system assigns to every input — including the vanishingly tiny probabilities it gives to wrong answers. To see why those tiny numbers matter, and how a temperature knob straight out of thermodynamics transfers them, we have to begin at the beginning.

The basic building blocks

Before the trick, the ground rules: what a classifier actually outputs (a distribution you already know from statistical mechanics), how it is normally trained, and why the most accurate systems — averaged committees of networks — are too expensive to serve.

A classifier outputs a Boltzmann distribution, not an answer

Take a network that must sort inputs into five classes. Its last layer produces one real number per class — the field calls each of these a logit, written zi — and you should read a logit as a negative energy. A final operation called the softmax converts logits into probabilities, qi=ezisumjezj, which is exactly the Boltzmann distribution e-E/kT from thermodynamics, evaluated at temperature T=1: lower energy means exponentially more occupation. Drag the middle logit upward and watch — every extra unit of logit multiplies its unnormalized weight by e≈2.718, and all the other bars must shrink to keep the total at exactly 1. So the network never answers with a single class; it answers with a whole distribution, and the shape of that distribution is where everything in this paper happens.

Normal training tells the network one thing per example: the right answer

How do we train this thing? The standard recipe uses a hard target: the true class gets probability 1 and every other class gets 0, and we minimize the cross-entropy C=-sumipilogqi between that target p and the network's output q. For a one-hot target this whole sum collapses to a single term, -logqk, where k is the correct class — try it: slide the correct logit down and the loss climbs without limit, slide it up and the loss falls toward zero. Gradient descent therefore pushes the correct logit up and every other logit down, and that is all it does. Each training example delivers essentially one piece of information — 'the answer is class k' — and says nothing about which wrong answers were nearly right.

Averaging many networks cancels their independent mistakes — at N times the cost

Here is the oldest trick for buying accuracy: train N copies of the same network from different random starting weights and average their output distributions — the field calls this committee an ensemble. Each copy lands in a different minimum and makes partly independent errors, so averaging cancels the idiosyncratic mistakes while reinforcing the shared, correct structure, the same variance-reduction you get by averaging N noisy measurements. Slide N up and watch the wrong squares in the averaged row wink out. In the paper's speech experiment, averaging 10 identical nets raises frame accuracy — the fraction of short audio slices assigned the correct speech-sound label — from 58.9% to 61.1%. But look at the cost meter: serving the ensemble costs 10 times the compute for every single prediction, which is why the paper calls it cumbersome, and why we need what comes next.

The new idea: teaching with the whole distribution

The committee is accurate but undeployable. The paper's move: its knowledge hides in the tiny wrong-answer probabilities, and a temperature knob amplifies them into a training signal that a small student network can learn from.

The tiny probabilities on wrong answers encode what the model knows

You would naturally say a model's knowledge is its weights — millions of numbers, useless to a network with a different shape. The paper says no: the knowledge is the mapping from inputs to full output distributions, and the interesting part hides in the wrong answers. A trained model shown a BMW might assign probability 10-4 to 'garbage truck' but 10-8 to 'carrot' — both tiny, but their ratio of ten thousand tells you how the model has organized its world. Click between these two handwritten 2s and watch the log-scale bars: one 2 gets 10-6 on '3' and 10-9 on '7', while the other, written differently, gets the reverse. The paper's community nicknamed this relational information dark knowledge, and a student trained to reproduce it inherits the teacher's way of generalizing — not just its answers.

Raising the temperature makes the tiny probabilities trainable

There is a problem: a probability of 10-6 contributes almost nothing to the cross-entropy gradient, so at T=1 all that dark knowledge is numerically invisible to a student. The fix is the same knob you would reach for in statistical mechanics — raise the temperature. Divide every logit by T before the softmax, qi=ezi/Tsumjezj/T, and the distribution flattens exactly as heating a physical system populates its excited states; drag the slider to the paper's MNIST setting of T=20 and the 10-6-scale bars rise to where a loss can feel them. Notice what temperature does not do: it adds no randomness and no information — the same logits go in, the ranking of classes never changes, it merely redistributes the loss's sensitivity toward the tail. The teacher's flattened distribution is what the paper calls soft targets; both teacher and student use the same high T during transfer, and at deployment the student runs at T=1, snapping back to a sharp, confident classifier.

The distillation loss: soft targets plus a small dose of true labels, rescaled by T²

Now assemble the actual algorithm, which the paper names distillation. The student computes one set of logits and pays two cross-entropies on them: one against the teacher's soft targets, with both networks run at the same temperature T, and one against the true hard label at T=1, given a considerably smaller weight — the real labels still help, but the teacher does most of the teaching. There is a trap hiding in the calculus: each division of the logits by T contributes a factor 1T to the gradient, so the soft term's gradient scales as 1T2 — at T=20 it would arrive 400 times weaker than the hard term, and raising the temperature would silently mute the teacher. Slide T with the correction off and watch the soft-gradient bar collapse; flip the T2 rescaling on and it holds steady. That one factor is what makes temperature a clean, independently tunable knob instead of a trade-off against the true labels.

At high temperature, distillation becomes squared-error matching of logits

What is distillation really asking the student to do? Differentiate the soft-target cross-entropy and you get ∂C∂zi=1T(qi-pi), where qi is the student's softened probability and pi the teacher's. When T is much larger than the logits, expand ez/T≈1+zT; with each example's logits shifted to zero mean, the gradient collapses to 1NT2(zi-vi) — exactly gradient descent on 12(zi-vi)2, the squared-error logit matching that Caruana's group had used before, now revealed as one extreme of a family. Slide T down and watch the curve peel away from the straight line precisely for logits far below the mean: moderate temperature pays less attention to the teacher's very negative logits, which the teacher's own training barely constrained and which are therefore noisy. That explains the paper's empirical finding — tiny students with 30 hidden units per layer do best at intermediate T≈2.5–4, while large students are insensitive to anything above T=8.

One soft-labeled example teaches far more than one hard-labeled example

Why should copying probabilities be so much more efficient than copying answers? Count the constraints: on a 14,000-class problem, a hard target pins down one fact per example — 'the answer is class k' — while a high-entropy soft target pins down 13,999 real-valued ratios, the teacher's whole opinion about that input. There is a second effect: soft targets are smooth functions of the input, so the gradient they generate varies far less from one training case to the next than the jumpy one-hot signal does. Slide the number of training examples and watch the two learning curves: the soft-target student climbs steeply and smoothly while the hard-target student crawls and jitters. More information per case plus lower gradient variance is why distilled students can train on much less data, with higher learning rates.

Head to head with the heavyweight

The recipe is in hand. Now we run it against the status quo on the paper's own benchmarks — handwritten digits, a production speech system, and a deliberately starved training set — and keep score with the paper's numbers.

On handwritten digits, the distilled student nearly matches its teacher

Time to keep score, starting on MNIST, the classic dataset of 10,000 handwritten test digits. The teacher is a big net — two layers of 1,200 units, trained with dropout (randomly silencing units during training, a strong regularizer) and with jittered inputs (images randomly shifted a pixel or two) — and it makes 67 test errors. A small student with two layers of 800 units, trained the ordinary hard-target way, makes 146. Press the third button: the same small student, trained with the teacher's soft targets at T=20 plus a small hard-target term, makes 74 errors — with no dropout, no jitter, no regularization of any kind beyond the soft targets themselves. Half the errors vanished because the student was told not just what each digit is, but what it almost is.

With soft targets, 3% of the data buys almost all of the performance

If soft targets really carry more information per example, starving the model of examples should hurt far less — and it does, dramatically. The 85-million-parameter speech model trained on all 700 million examples reaches 58.9% frame accuracy on held-out test data. Flip to hard targets on just 3% of the data (about 20 million examples) and press play: training accuracy soars to 67.3% while test accuracy peaks and then falls to 44.5% — the model is memorizing its small training set, a failure called overfitting, and you must halt training early ('early stopping') to salvage anything. Now flip to soft targets on the same 3%: the test curve climbs to 57.0% and simply flattens there, no early stopping needed. Each soft target constrains the model's entire output distribution on that example, forbidding the pathological weight settings that memorize the training set — the teacher's generalization behavior itself is being transmitted.

A student that has never seen a 3 learns to recognize 3s

Here is the sharpest proof that soft targets transfer structure, not just labels. Delete every 3 from the student's training set, so it never sees a single positive example — you would expect the 3-class to be unlearnable, its probability pinned near zero for every input. But every 2, 5, and 8 in the training set arrives with the teacher's soft target attached, and each one carries a small, structured probability for '3', quietly mapping out where 3s live in output space; the student ends up making only 206 test errors in total, 133 of them on the 1,010 test 3s. Its one systematic defect is a bias — a constant added to the 3-class logit — that sits too low, since training never rewarded saying '3'. Drag that single number up by 3.5 and total errors fall to 109, with the student now getting 98.6% of the 3s it never saw correct; even a training set containing only 7s and 8s yields just 13.2% test error after bias correction.

One distilled network keeps the 10-model ensemble's gains at a tenth of the cost

Now the industrial test, on the acoustic model behind Android voice search: an 8-layer, 85-million-parameter network classifying each audio frame into one of 14,000 speech-sound states. Ten copies trained from different random seeds and averaged — the cumbersome ensemble from Part 1 — lift frame accuracy from 58.9% to 61.1% and cut word error rate (the fraction of words the full speech system transcribes wrongly) from 10.9% to 10.7%. Press the third button: a single network of the same size, distilled from the ensemble's averaged soft targets at T=2 with a hard-target weight of 0.5, reaches 60.8% frame accuracy and the very same 10.7% word error rate. That is over 80% of the ensemble's accuracy gain and 100% of its word-error gain, delivered at one-tenth the cost per prediction. The knowledge of ten networks now fits in one — this is the paper's motivating problem, solved.

Why now: past the committee entirely

Distillation solves deployment, but at Google scale even training the committee became infeasible — one baseline took six months. The same soft-target ideas yield a parallel army of specialist networks that trains in days, pointing at where this line of work goes next.

When labels number in the thousands, train one generalist plus many independent specialists

Distillation fixed serving cost, but at Google's scale even training became the wall: on JFT, an internal dataset of 100 million images spanning 15,000 classes, the single baseline network took six months, so a 10-copy ensemble would take years. The paper's answer: train one broad model — the generalist — and then 61 specialists, each responsible for roughly 300 classes the generalist tends to confuse (mushroom species, say, or sports cars); a standard clustering procedure called K-means, applied to the covariance of the generalist's predictions, finds these confusable groups without needing any true labels, and we will treat it as a well-tested black box. Each specialist starts from a copy of the generalist's weights — inheriting its low-level features and resisting overfitting — trains on a half-and-half mix of its own classes and random data, and lumps all other classes into a single 'dustbin' output. Crucially, this is not a jointly trained committee with a gating network: assignments are fixed up front, so every specialist trains completely independently, in parallel, in a few days. Slide the coverage: overall top-1 accuracy (the single best guess being correct) rises from 25.0% to 26.1%, and for test classes covered by nine or more specialists the gain reaches +14–17%.

At test time, one distribution is fitted to agree with the generalist and every active specialist

One puzzle remains: each specialist only speaks about its ~300 classes plus a dustbin, so how do their voices combine into one answer over all 15,000 classes? For each image, the generalist proposes its top classes, which wakes every specialist whose subset overlaps them; then we search for the single full distribution q=softmax(z) that is simultaneously closest to the generalist's opinion and to every awake specialist's. 'Closest' means minimizing KL divergence — a standard measure of the gap between two probability distributions, zero when they match — so the objective is minqKL(pg,q)+summKL(pm,q); when comparing q against a specialist, all of q's mass on that specialist's non-special classes is summed into its dustbin slot. There is no closed-form answer in general, so press play and watch gradient descent run on the logits of q for this one image, the KL total falling until the curves settle. In the special case of a single shared probability per class, the solution reduces to a simple arithmetic or geometric mean of the models — reassurance that this machinery is the principled generalization of plain averaging, which is where this whole story began.
