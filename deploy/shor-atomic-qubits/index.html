How 10,000 Atoms Could Break the Internet's Locks

The codes that guard your bank account rest on one bet: that no computer on Earth can factor a certain 2,048-bit number before the sun burns out. A new kind of machine — one that computes with single atoms held in laser beams, atoms it can pick up and carry around mid-calculation — threatens that bet, and a new paper argues it needs only about 10,000 atoms instead of the million everyone expected. That factor of a hundred is the difference between a distant fantasy and a machine whose parts already exist in laboratories today. To understand the claim honestly, we have to begin at the beginning: what a quantum bit actually is, why it is so terribly fragile, and how you protect information you are forbidden to look at.

The basic building blocks

We start from the one thing you already know — an ordinary bit — and follow it step by step to the wall that has stalled an entire field: protecting quantum information is so expensive that breaking real encryption seemed to need a million-part machine.

A quantum bit can lean between 0 and 1

Suppose you have a bit — a switch that reads 0 or 1, nothing else. Nature offers something stranger: store information in a single atom's internal state, and you can prepare it leaning part way between 0 and 1, a genuine blend of both at once — physicists call this blend a superposition, and the whole object a qubit. Slide the lean and watch the readout odds follow: tilt the superposition three-quarters toward 1, and reading it gives a 1 three times out of four. A qubit is not a faster bit; its power comes later, when many qubits hold many possibilities at once and those superpositions interfere like overlapping waves. For now, hold onto the picture: the information lives in the superposition — the angle of the lean — not in a definite 0 or 1.

Each operation corrupts a qubit about once in a thousand tries

That delicate superposition is stored in something microscopic, and the world will not leave it alone: a stray photon, a vibration, a slightly imperfect laser pulse — any of these knocks the arrow to a random angle. In today's best hardware, each two-qubit operation goes wrong about 1 time in 1,000, an error rate we write as p≈0.1%. That sounds excellent until you multiply: the chance of surviving N operations unscathed is (1-0.001)N, and by N≈700 you are down to a coin flip. Drag the slider out to 10,000 operations and watch the survival odds collapse toward zero. A serious computation needs billions of steps — so raw qubits, on their own, are hopeless.

A quantum computer can factor the numbers that guard the internet

Your bank's lock is a math problem: multiply two enormous secret primes and publish the product, and anyone can lock a message but only the prime-holder can unlock it. Factoring that 2,048-bit product with the best classical methods would take longer than the age of the universe — slide the key size up and watch the classical time bar shoot off the chart. In 1994 Peter Shor showed that a quantum computer, using the interference of superpositions — many held-at-once possibilities, can find a hidden repeating pattern in these problems exponentially faster; it does not try all keys at once, it exploits that specific structure — which is why newer schemes without it are believed safe. The cost is counted in a basic three-bit step called the Toffoli gate: flip one bit only if two others are both 1. Breaking one 2,048-bit key takes about 6.5 billion Toffoli gates; a 256-bit elliptic-curve key, the other common lock, takes only 70–90 million.

You can find an error without looking at the data

For ordinary bits, protection is easy: store each bit three times and take a majority vote. Quantum information forbids this — you cannot copy a superposition, and reading one destroys it, so it seems protection is impossible. The solution is beautiful: spread one protected qubit — a logical qubit — across several physical ones, and never ask any qubit what it holds; instead, ask each pair of neighbors a single yes/no question: 'are you two the same, or different?' That comparison is a parity check, and it is safe — learning that two qubits agree tells you nothing about what they hold, just as learning that a couple wore matching outfits tells you nothing about the color. But an error that flips one qubit makes it suddenly disagree with both of its neighbors: flip any qubit below and watch exactly the two checks touching it switch to 'different!', pinpointing the culprit while the protected data stays sealed. Run every check each cycle — 1 millisecond in this paper — and errors are caught as fast as they arrive.

Each step of code distance multiplies reliability

How much protection do the checks buy? A code has a distance, d: the minimum number of simultaneous physical errors needed to silently corrupt the stored information. For the code to fail, roughly d/2 independent errors must land in exactly the wrong pattern, so the logical failure rate falls like Plogical∝pd/2 — each increase in distance multiplies your reliability rather than adding to it. Slide d up and watch the failure rate plunge down the logarithmic axis: at a physical error rate p of 0.1%, the paper's largest code, with distance up to 24, reaches about 10-11 failures per cycle — a hundred-million-fold better than the raw hardware. One catch, and it is the hinge of everything: flip p above a critical threshold value and the magic runs backwards — bigger codes get worse. Staying below threshold is a hardware achievement, and neutral-atom experiments sit 2× below it today, with a path to 10×.

The standard plan needs about a million qubits to break one key

Now let us pay the bill. The standard protection scheme, the surface code, arranges physical qubits on a flat grid where each talks only to its immediate neighbors — perfect for a rigid chip, but the grid patch protects exactly one logical qubit, at a cost of hundreds to thousands of physical qubits apiece — an encoding rate, protected qubits per physical qubit, of just 0.1% to 4%. Shor's algorithm needs 1,200 to 1,500 logical qubits, plus factories for special helper states we will meet later. Drag the slider and watch the patches tile the floor: by the time you reach RSA-2048's requirement, the counter reads about 1,000,000 physical qubits. Today's largest quantum processors hold a few hundred. The obstacle is not the algorithm — it is this overhead, and it is exactly what the paper attacks.

The new machine: atoms you can carry

Now we meet the paper's hardware and its central trick. When qubits are single atoms that can be physically moved, you can wire together partners that are far apart — and that one freedom changes the economics of protection entirely.

Atoms held in laser beams can be carried to any partner

Remember what a parity check does: it compares qubits. Here is the problem — on a fixed chip, a qubit can only talk to the neighbors it is wired to, so comparing two distant qubits means a bucket brigade: pass the information through every qubit in between, each handoff a fresh 1-in-1,000 chance of error. That confines chips to checks among adjacent qubits, and the next scene shows that this confinement is exactly what makes protection so expensive. This paper's hardware dissolves the problem: each qubit is a single atom held in a steerable laser beam — an optical tweezer — and to compare two far-apart atoms you simply pick one up and set it beside the other: one move, one comparison, however far apart they started. Slide the separation below and compare the two costs yourself. The price is pace — about 1 millisecond per operation cycle, a thousand times slower than rigid chips — and the whole paper is a wager that the freedom to rewire is worth far more than the speed it gives up.

Far-reaching checks let many qubits share one set of guards

Why does reach matter so much? Think of the guarding qubits — the redundancy — as security guards. When checks reach only neighbors, the block splits into fenced-off patches, and every logical qubit must hire its own private crew; none of the guards can be shared. Let the checks reach anywhere and one communal web of guards watches over many logical qubits at once — the same guard helps protect dozens of tenants, the way one alarm system serves a whole building instead of one per apartment. Toggle the reach below on a toy block of 18 atoms and count it yourself: fenced into neighbor-only patches, those 18 atoms protect just 2 logical qubits; rewoven into one far-reaching web, the same 18 atoms protect 6. Same atoms, same number of checks — three times the protection, purely from reach. The next scene pays this out at the paper's full scale.

Long-range checks pack 1,480 protected qubits into 5,278 atoms

Now the payoff at full scale. The paper's workhorse code weaves 5,278 atoms into one communal web protecting 1,480 logical qubits at distance 24 — nearly 30 protected qubits per 100 atoms, where the neighbor-only surface code managed between 0.1 and 4. Buying those same 1,480 logical qubits at the same distance with fenced patches would cost 852,480 atoms: 161 times more, for the same one-in-a-hundred-billion per-cycle failure rate. And the web stays tame — each check still touches only about 10 qubits, so no single measurement becomes unwieldy. Slide the check reach from 'neighbors only' toward 'anywhere' and watch the atom bill collapse from 852,480 down to 5,278: that single collapse is where 'a million qubits, someday' becomes 'ten thousand atoms'.

The machine splits into a big library and a small processor

Dense packing has a price. In the big block, the 1,480 logical qubits are woven together by shared checks — wonderful for safekeeping, terrible for access: you cannot work on one book without disturbing the whole shelf. So the machine splits in two like a closed-stack library — and from here on, think of each stored logical qubit as a book: the big block is the stacks, where books are only ever stored, never worked on. Beside it sits a small processor — a separate code holding just 10 to 148 logical qubits — and every actual computation step happens there. Pick any book below and the rule is always the same: to be computed on, it must first travel to the processor. The trick that makes that trip possible comes next.

Two atoms can be prepared to always agree

One more quantum ingredient, and it is the strangest one. Two atoms can be prepared together — entangled — so that their readouts are locked to each other: read both and you get 0-and-0 or 1-and-1, never a mismatch, no matter how far apart the atoms have since been carried. Neither atom secretly knows its answer in advance — each readout alone is a fair coin flip — yet the two flips always land the same way. Press 'measure both' below and tally the outcomes: fifty-fifty between 00 and 11, and never once 01 or 10. Hold onto this: a shared entangled pair is a resource — and in the next scene, the machine spends such pairs to move quantum states around.

Books travel to the processor by quantum teleportation

How does a book get from the stacks to the processor without being read? Through a standard, well-tested procedure called quantum teleportation — and we will not explain its inner workings here; honestly, they take quantum arithmetic to appreciate. What matters for this paper is teleportation's price tag, and that much you can watch: each fetch consumes one freshly entangled pair of helper atoms — the matched pairs from the last scene — takes about 16 milliseconds of repeated measurements, and sends only ordinary classical bits across the machine. Two guarantees come with it: the book's contents are never read along the way, and the original is always destroyed — a quantum state can be moved, never copied. Press fetch and watch the machine pay the price and collect the goods.

One shared pool of helpers serves every fetch

Notice what the fetch consumed: a crew of helper atoms, busy for about sixteen milliseconds, then released. Here is the final economy of movability. Because any atom can be carried anywhere, the machine keeps just one communal crew — 900 to 1,900 helpers, 10 to 20 percent of all its atoms — and rearranges the same crew, fetch after fetch, to serve whichever book is called next. Toggle the comparison below: a rigid chip, whose helpers cannot move, would need a dedicated measuring rig wired in beside every one of the 1,480 books, and the rigs would dwarf the library itself. One roaming crew versus fourteen hundred parked ones — that is why the helper overhead stays a footnote instead of becoming the bill.

The one gate the codes cannot do runs on manufactured fuel

There is a hole in the armor. Error-correcting codes natively support only a limited family of operations called Clifford gates — necessary, but a machine running only those can be efficiently simulated by an ordinary computer, so it has no quantum power at all. The missing ingredient is exactly the Toffoli gate we count Shor's algorithm in, and it is supplied indirectly: prepare a special three-qubit resource called a magic state offline, then splice it into the computation with the same teleportation move the machine uses for fetching books, consuming it like fuel — 'magic' is a term of art, not hype, naming the specific resource that elevates the machine. Raw magic states are dirty, so step through the factory: first 'cultivation' grows small |T⟩ states with error near 10-6, then batches of eight run through a distillation circuit that outputs one CCZ state with error near 10-10 — clean enough for billions of gates. The factory brews these faster, on average, than the computation burns them.

The head-to-head: why 10,000 beats a million

With the machine assembled, we can run the paper's arithmetic against the standard plan: the same protection from 161 times fewer parts, a failure budget that closes, and a menu of runtimes from 264 days down to 10.

A months-long run tolerates about one failure per hundred billion cycles

Now one line of arithmetic decides everything. For the whole run to succeed with 90% probability, the expected number of block failures must stay below about 0.1. Each Toffoli occupies a few hundred cycles — about 19 fetch-lengths, τToff≈19×16 rounds, and in every cycle the memory can fail with probability PL, so the tolerable gate count is nToff=log(0.9)τToff·log(1−PL). Slide PL down and watch the budget grow: you must reach about 10-11 before the markers for ECC-256's 9×107 Toffolis and RSA-2048's 6.5×109 fall inside the green zone. If 10-11 sounded like absurd overkill back in the distance scene, here is why it is not: a months-long run at 1 millisecond per cycle accumulates around 1010 cycles, so one-in-a-hundred-billion per cycle only barely keeps success likely. Working backwards, that demands physical error rates p between 0.048% and 0.093% — near-term plausible — and code sizes that total 9,739 to 13,255 atoms.

Extra atoms buy speed: 264 days can become 10

The lean 10,000-atom machine runs Toffoli gates one at a time, each costing an amortized 10–72 surgery cycles at 1 millisecond apiece — so ECC-256's 90 million Toffolis take 52 to 1,000 days depending on processor size, around 264 days in the paper's representative design. The paper attacks that serial bottleneck twice: mathematically, swapping ripple-carry adders, whose n-bit additions need about n to 2n sequential Toffoli layers, for carry-lookahead adders needing only about 4·logn; and physically, distilling and consuming P magic states simultaneously through parallel surgery. Slide P up and watch the trade: more parallel machinery means more atoms, less time — ECC-256 at P=130 costs 26,000 qubits and about 10 days, RSA-2048 at P=1,160 costs 102,000 qubits and about 97 days. Notice the twist: the 'modern' elliptic-curve locks fall sooner than RSA not because of hardware but because their keys demand about 70× fewer Toffoli gates.

Why now, and what comes next

A resource estimate only matters if the hardware is real. Here is what laboratories have already demonstrated, piece by piece — and why the remaining gap is integration, not invention.

Every ingredient has already worked in a laboratory

Why should you believe any of this is near? Because the paper invents no new physics — it re-budgets capabilities already shown. Click through the checklist: arrays of 6,100 coherent atoms have been trapped, comfortably past the 9,739–13,255 the lean machine needs; patterns of 360,000 tweezers have been demonstrated, enough even for the 102,000-atom fast variant; full fault-tolerant operation — the stitching, checking, and correcting of earlier scenes — has run on about 500 qubits; error rates sit 2× below the critical threshold, with a credible path to 10×, against the required p of 0.048% to 0.093%; and readout as fast as 1 microsecond points at recovering the 1-millisecond cycle's lost speed. No single machine has combined them yet — that is the honest gap. But it is an engineering gap, and it is why a claim that once meant 'a million qubits, someday' now reads 'ten thousand atoms, days to months of runtime' — and why the world is racing to encryption without hidden periodic patterns before the atoms finish moving.
